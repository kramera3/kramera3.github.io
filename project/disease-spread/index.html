Macroscale spread of disease

 

Part of my computational research examines methods for modeling spread on networks. This work focuses on the past and future spread of white-nose syndrome, a devastating disease of bats, in North America. By modeling the epidemic on a network, we have shown that the risk of spread between counties depends on a combination of distance, cave density and climate (Maher et al. 2012). These results have contributed to management decisions for affected species, and generated valuable insights about how the spatial spread of Ebola virus in West Africa can be predicted by evaluating alternative epidemic paths. My current work addresses two important problems: simplifying the complex networks that often result from these models and coupling processes at the local scale to macro-scale spread on the network. This will contribute to forecasts of future spread of white-nose syndrome and be transferable to systems from zoonotic disease to aquatic invasive species.
